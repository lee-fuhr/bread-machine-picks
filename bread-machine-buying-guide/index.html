How to choose the right bread machine

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Most home bakers get the best results from a mid-priced horizontal loaf machine that bakes a 2-pound loaf, because it produces standard-shaped sandwich slices and offers enough programmable features to adapt to different flours and recipes. For tight countertops, a vertical machine saves space but yields taller, pull-apart slices. Skip super-cheap machines unless you are certain you will use it only occasionally.

What size bread machine should you buy?

Home bread machines typically bake loaf sizes of 1, 1.5, or 2 pounds. A 1-pound loaf is fine for a single person or a couple, but the small loaf height can lead to dense results because the kneading paddles struggle in a compact pan. Most experienced owners and published reviews recommend a 2-pound machine for a family of four or anyone who wants room to experiment with whole-grain or add-in recipes. A 2-pound machine gives you more headroom in the pan, which allows the dough to rise properly and creates a softer crumb. It also handles wetter doughs (like those for whole wheat or rye) better than smaller models. The only downside is counter space: a typical 2-pound horizontal machine is about a foot long and 8 inches wide, so measure your counter before buying.

Horizontal vs. vertical loaf pans: what’s the trade-off?

Horizontal machines produce a traditional rectangular loaf that slices neatly into sandwich-size pieces. The paddle leaves a small hole in the bottom of the loaf, but the overall shape is what most people expect. Because the pan sits in the machine horizontally, the heating elements wrap around it evenly, which usually leads to more consistent browning. Vertical machines, sometimes called loaf-shaped pans, produce a tall, rounded loaf that slices into smaller, more irregular pieces. The upside is a much smaller footprint on your counter, some vertical models are only 6 inches wide. The trade-off is that the crust can be thicker on one side, and the slices aren’t ideal for a standard toaster. If you mostly make pull-apart bread or aren’t fussy about uniform sandwich slices, a vertical machine is perfectly fine and saves space.

Which features are worth paying extra for?

A delay timer (usually up to 13 or 15 hours) is genuinely useful if you want to wake up to fresh bread or have it ready when you walk in the door. Crust control settings (light, medium, dark) let you dial in your preference, and this feature is common even on mid-price machines. A custom or manual cycle that lets you set your own knead, rise, and bake times is a major advantage if you ever bake with non-wheat flours or want to tweak a recipe. Other nice-to-haves include a keep-warm function (most machines have it) and a viewing window. A window is handy but can fog up during the rise; some machines have a small light inside to help you peek. Avoid paying extra for “jam” or “yogurt” cycles unless you genuinely plan to use them, most bread machine jams are thin and require a second cooking step on the stove.

Do you really need a machine with a gluten-free cycle?

Many modern bread machines include a dedicated gluten-free program that shortens the rise time and adjusts the baking temperature, because gluten-free doughs require a different hydration and often need less kneading. If you or someone in your household has celiac disease or a gluten sensitivity, that dedicated cycle can make a real difference in texture. The bake is also typically shorter to prevent the crust from over-browning while the center finishes cooking. That said, you can bake good gluten-free bread in a machine without a dedicated cycle by using a custom program or the whole-wheat cycle and manually stopping the rise early. The dedicated cycle just removes the guesswork. If you bake gluten-free at least once a week, it’s worth prioritizing a machine with that preset. If you only bake occasionally, a cheaper model with a manual cycle will work fine.

How to judge build quality and reliability

Look for a machine with a sturdy metal or heavy-duty plastic body, thin plastic panels can flex during kneading, which leads to vibration and uneven mixing. A nonstick bread pan that feels substantial and has a strong central shaft for the paddle usually lasts longer. The lid hinge is a common failure point on budget models; examine owner reviews for complaints about lids breaking after a few months. Another reliability indicator is the motor. Most machines use a small DC motor that handles the load okay, but machines over about 600 watts tend to have more torque for stiffer doughs. You can’t easily inspect the motor beforehand, but brands with strong reputations for small appliances (such as Cuisinart, Zojirushi, or Breville) generally use higher-quality components. A warranty of at least 2 years (and preferably 3) is a good sign.

Should you buy a budget or premium bread machine?

Budget-priced bread machines (roughly though we won’t give a specific number) often lack crust control, have limited programming, and use lighter pans that can warp over time. They’re fine for someone who wants to try bread making without a big investment, but be prepared for less consistent results and a shorter lifespan. Many owners report that budget machines become noisy after a year of regular use. Premium machines (generally a few hundred dollars) offer superior temperature regulation, stronger motors, and quieter operation. They also tend to have more thoughtful touches like collapsible kneading paddles (so the loaf doesn’t have a big hole in the bottom) and longer timers. For frequent bakers, anyone making bread one to three times a week, the extra cost pays off in reliability and better loaves. A mid-range machine from a reputable brand, costing somewhere between the two, offers the best balance of features and durability for most households.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use my own recipe in a bread machine?

Yes, but only if the machine has a manual or custom cycle that lets you control knead time, rise time, and bake time. Pre-programmed cycles assume a specific ratio of wet to dry ingredients, so a random all-purpose recipe may over- or under-proof. Stick to recipes written for bread machines or use the dough cycle and shape the loaf yourself.

How long does a typical bread cycle take?

A standard white bread cycle usually takes 3 to 4 hours from start to finish. Whole wheat cycles often take longer because of the extra kneading and longer rise times. Many machines also have a rapid cycle that finishes in about 1.5 hours, but the resulting loaf tends to be denser and less flavorful.

Does a bread machine require bread flour or will all-purpose work?

You can use all-purpose flour, but bread flour’s higher protein content (12–14 percent) produces a better rise and chewier texture. Whole wheat, rye, and other specialty flours work in most machines if you follow recipes that adjust the liquid. Avoid very low-protein flours like cake flour unless you are following a specific gluten-free blend.

Is it possible to make dough only and bake in the oven?

Absolutely. Almost every bread machine has a dough-only cycle that kneads and sometimes proofs the dough but doesn’t bake it. This is a common way to use a machine for homemade pizza, rolls, or artisan loaves that you shape and bake in a conventional oven. You get the convenience of machine kneading without limiting yourself to the machine’s baking chamber.

How do I clean a bread machine properly?

Unplug the machine and let the pan cool completely before removing it. Wash the nonstick pan and kneading paddle in warm, soapy water with a soft sponge, never use abrasive scrubbers. Wipe the interior of the machine with a damp cloth to remove any flour dust or crumbs. The lid and heating elements should only be wiped; never submerge the machine body in water or run it through a dishwasher.

What about yeast: active dry vs. instant?

Instant yeast (sometimes sold as rapid-rise or bread machine yeast) is the best choice for bread machines because it can be mixed directly with the flour without proofing first. Active dry yeast needs to be dissolved in warm water, which adds an extra step and can be tricky if your machine has a timed delay start. Most machine manuals recommend instant yeast for the easiest results.

In shortThe best bread machine for you isn’t the most expensive one, it’s the one whose size, loaf shape, and feature set match your counter space, baking frequency, and preferred bread style. Start with a well-reviewed mid-range machine from a brand with a track record for small appliances, and don’t be afraid to use the dough cycle for oven-baked recipes. With a little practice, you’ll be turning out loaves that beat anything from the grocery store.
